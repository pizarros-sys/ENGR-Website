Design Project One

For my nametag print, I copied the nametag file into Prusa, edited it to add my name, exported it onto a USB drive, and printed it out.

For my torture test, I selected the calibration cat to print, copied the file into Prusa, exported it onto an USB drive, and printed it out. I had to print it a second time, because during the first attempt, the plastic filament snapped in the middle of printing. The second print was successful, but it was mildly under-extruded and has trailing strings on the bottom. Both cats were consistently sized. From this, I can conclude that more detailed and complex prints are more prone to errors such as issues with extrusion, trailing strings, and broken filaments.

For my accessibility print, I choose a light switch extension lever, designed by Devon Smith and Makers Making Change for people with issues gripping small objects. I copied the file into Prusa, exported it onto a USB drive, and printed it out. It appears to be functional, but did not fit on the light switch when I tried it. Perhaps it was intended for a different size of light switch, or I made a mistake while copying the file. If I intended to use this print, I would measure the light switches in question and make sure the print was sized correctly to fit them.

Overall, I am now fairly confident in my ability to edit and export files from Prusa and use the 3D printers. I am also fairly confident in my grasp of basic HTML editing, although my website still could use improvement to be more aesthetically pleasing and intuitively designed.

Design Project Two

I sketched out a design for a water bottle holder that could be clipped onto a bag. Ideally, it would reduce misplaced bottles and increase accessibility for one-handed use, as it is easier to carry one bag with an attachment than to carry a bag and and a water bottle. This design demonstrates the principle of task unification, as it improves upon the carabiner clip to allow it to hold an item as well as attach two items. It was PLA only, approximately palm sized, and compatible with bottled drinks I often purchase, but I had some concerns about the supports needed to print it.

The design ended up looking a little different than I envisioned, due to the specific STL files I found. I combined a carabiner clip with a bottle holder, exported the file from Tinkercad to Prusa, uploaded it onto a thumb drive, and printed it. I had to print this design twice, as it turned out to need more supports than I had assigned to it.

My first attempt at printing revealed a sizing issue: the holder was not large enough to hold any beverages I own. Therefore, it could not be used. I resized my print by a factor of 1.25 in Onshape, exported it to Prusa, uploaded it onto a thumb drive, and printed it.

My second print was the right size to conveniently hold a beverage, and was entirely successful before I accidentally broke the carabiner clip. In a future print, I would attempt to make the carabiner clip less prone to breakage, perhaps by using a different pattern.

I learned that I find editing dimensions is easier in Tinkercad than in Onshape, especially if I'm working without a mouse, but the merge mechanics in both are about equally difficult. Also, Tinkercad sometimes claims designs that are valid in Onshape are incomplete, and I'm not sure why.

Design Project Three

I designed a simple chassis, intended to be easily slid on and off for battery changes, with a gap at the front to avoid obstructing the LEDs and sensors and another at the back to avoid obstructing the wheels and power button. My first print failed and was too small.

I printed again a fourth time, at 110% length, 140% width, and 150% height.

Design Project Four

My partner selected and printed the Phoenix V2 Hand, with the palm and demo bars from Phoenix V1. I did most of the disassembly of the supports, and I conducted the first stages of construction. Once I understood the process, the assembly stages were relatively intuitive: connect the joints with the pins. I had some difficulties, however, assembling the fingers in the right order: from the index and middle, then the ring, then the pinkie. I first tried the other way around, and couldn't get the pins to fit. Once I assembled the hand correctedly, I connected the oval notches in the finger joints with rubber bands.

My partner finished the assembly, wiring the fingers to the mechanism at the base of the prosthetic. The hand has a functional grip and the wrist-driven mechanism works, but is not suited to fine motor control. Ideally it would have some kind of internal padding to make it more comfortable for the end user, as the plastic joints are not pleasant to be in contact with for extended periods of time.

Norman's "Design Thinking" argues on the importance of solving the correct problem, rather than the most seemingly obvious one, and-Chock's Design Justice discusses the importance of designing for the needs of real end users, rather than making assumptions on their behalf. Open-source, volunteer-driven limb prosthetics such as the Phoenix V2 Hand are inexpensive and successful at visually imitating the human hand. However, the problem facing end users of limb prosthetics is generally not the "obvious" problem, the lack of a hand, but the inability to perform various daily tasks that require two hands. Our print is successful at enabiling a user to grip non-fragile objects large enough not to slip through the prosthetic's fingers but small enough to fit in their hand. However, there are a myriad of other daily tasks where this model would not be useful.

Design Project Five Write-Up

Individuals with only one functional hand lack a practical way to tie standard shoelaces because existing solutions fail to provide full autonomy and are too conspicuous for formal wear. The target customer we are catering to is someone with one functioning hand. We want something both a professional can wear to work and a kid could wear to school with swagger. There are 3 million people worldwide who have an arm amputation, which makes up 30% of all amputees, with 2.4 million of arm amputees living in developing countries (OSU.edu). This highlights the need for something accessible and cheap. According to Hospital News, “In North America, there are over 58 million people of all ages experiencing limited hand mobility, hand and arm weakness and difficulty grasping and controlling objects, because of a medical condition or injury.”

We contacted an occupational therapist, Meaghan Myers, who said there are 3 main difficult steps for tying your shoes with one hand: crossing to make an x, making the bunny ear for the loop, and pulling the string through the loop. This was helpful, but we were hoping to understand the process better and with more detail. We have not yet spoken to someone with first hand experience, but we plan to later on in the process.

We researched the existing solutions and 3D printed some open source designs to better understand their strengths and weaknesses, using the define thinking method of defining the problem. We also simply tried to tie our shoes one-handed and then used Youtube tutorials, using the design thinking mode of empathizing with the end users' potential experiences.

The 3 open source designs we printed were a tension block, a shoelace clip, and a shoelace guide.To make the tension block stronger, we used PETG filament and changed the infill to gyroid. The cam (the teardrop shaped part) rotates so you can clamp one lace underneath it to create tension. You keep one foot on the flat bottom throughout the process. The peg on the top is used for creating one of the loops.

STRENGTHS: the teardrop shape was effective in pulling the strings taut and having a small peg for the loop to go around was helpful. WEAKNESSES: bulky and not easily portable, requires very long laces.

The other two prints were printed in PLA. The shoelace clip has three parts that fit into each other. You thread the laces through the 3 holes on either side and tuck the remaining length of the laces into the shoe. This device stays on your shoe permanently. To get your shoe on and off, you press on one side which releases the other to loosen the laces.

STRENGTHS: easy to use one-handed once set-up, avoids having to retie your shoes every day. WEAKNESSES: large and conspicuous, uncomfortable with the extra laces tucked in.

The shoelace guide was successful in our test. You thread both laces through the two parallel holes, and then thread one lace through the third hole to make a loop. You then make a loop with the other lace and tie the laces together. To untie your shoelaces, you pull the lace out of the third hole and unpick the knot.

STRENGTHS: discreet, inconspicuous, portable, allows traditional shoelace knots. WEAKNESSES: time-consuming to use, easy to lose.

Problem Definition & Brainstorming: In 3 minutes, each member listed as many aspects of the shoe tying process as we could think of. This was helpful to get the ball rolling in terms of brainstorming and collaboration. We already started to see some common ideas, such as the importance of gripping, tension, and efficiency.

Next, we started our structured “Thematic Analysis”: writing down words, phrases, quotes, etc. that stood out to us from research. We answered the guiding questions below.

○ Take a good long look across your interviews and other research. Have any patterns emerged? Is there a compelling insight you heard again and again? The main challenges are maneuvering and looping the string into a knot and the lack of a second point of control to manage tension.

○ A consistent problem the people you’re designing for face? Devices, modified laces, or alternative shoes focus on replacing tying altogether rather than supporting the actual process. People should be able to choose any shoe they want.

○ What feels significant? Autonomy, efficiency, and pride

○ What surprised you? Our interviewee mentioned one of the hardest things to do would be crossing to make an x, which we did not consider to be a problem.

Repeating themes: autonomy, freedom of choice, and efficiency.

Based on our thematic analysis, we came up with 3 insight statements: A person without a working hand feels burdensome when someone else needs to tie their shoes for them. A person without a working hand feels limited when they go shoe shopping.A person without a working hand desires efficiency when getting ready in the morning.

We then further developed those into 3 “How Might We” Statements. How might we provide someone with additional autonomy in their daily lives? How might we open up the opportunity for disabled individuals to purchase typical, non-modified items at a store? How might we speed up the process of getting ready in the morning for people with limited dexterity?

We brainstormed ideas under our three “How Might We” statements. We put down any and all ideas, even the ones that were obscure or infeasible. Then, we came up with 3 top ideas based on the overlapping thoughts from the three sections. The goal was to choose ideas that were portable, practical, work with any laced shoe, and quick. Our top ideas were: 1. Hook on side of shoe: discrete, portable, minimalist, cheap. 2. Clamp: basic, mobile, practical, creates tension. 3. Guiding device: stays on shoe, not as novel, good for teaching.

The idea we decided to work on was a modification of the “hook on side of shoe” thought. Rather than a hook, we realized we could use a simple post. You can stick the post through one of the top holes of your shoe, and use it to wrap the loop around. It could be easily portable with a keychain or it can remain in your shoe throughout the day. We also realized that kids could put a small design on the end of it, sort of like jibbitz for Crocs, to customize it. The “snazzy shoez” were born!

We created a storyboard to show the steps needed to successfully tie your shoe with our final selected idea, the Snazzy Shoez. We do not currently have any printed design, but we used an M3*20 screw as a prototype; it worked really well and is pictured in the storyboard.

Ground rules of our group project: These are the terms of group collaboration that we agree on as a team. Everyone should openly participate during meetings. We will listen to all ideas respectfully to create a space where everyone feels comfortable participating. Everyone should keep active in the slack to keep moving our project forward. We want to keep our group dynamic lighthearted. We think that a good dynamic will help keep the project fun and moving forward. We should make sure to respectfully keep each other on track, but still enjoy the process! If conflict or problems arise, respectful communication should be the first step. We want to identify strengths, divide the workload during meetings, and have our part of the work done by our next meeting or decided due date.

Roles of project members: depends on what we need to get done each week, but will be very collaborative, not hierarchical.

Meeting schedule: Monday 4:30pm weekly.
